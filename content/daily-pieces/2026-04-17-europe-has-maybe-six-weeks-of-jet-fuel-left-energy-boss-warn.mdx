## Six Weeks of Fuel

Europe has six weeks of jet fuel left.

That sounds alarming. It's also completely normal. This is how modern supply chains work by design. Airlines fly, cargo moves, people travel—and all of it runs on inventories measured in weeks, not months. The headline reveals something most of us never see: the gap between the abundance we experience and the fragility underneath it. The question isn't "why so little?" but "why does everything work this way?"

## The Just-in-Time Revolution

Start with what inventory actually costs.

Say you run a refinery. You produce jet fuel every day. You could store six months of output—tanks full of fuel, ready for any disruption. But those tanks cost money to build. The fuel sitting in them ties up capital you could invest elsewhere. If demand patterns shift or specs change, you're stuck with product you can't sell. Storage isn't free insurance. It's expensive inventory.

So you don't store six months. You store three days. Maybe five.

This is just-in-time inventory. You produce what you need, when you need it, in the quantity you need. Your suppliers deliver on schedule. Your customers take delivery on schedule. The system runs like a pulse—steady, predictable, efficient. A refinery produces, a pipeline moves it, a depot holds it for days, trucks deliver it to airports, planes refuel and fly. Total time from refinery to engine: less than two weeks in normal conditions.

Six weeks of supply doesn't mean six weeks sitting in one place. It means six weeks flowing through the entire system—refineries producing, pipelines moving, depots cycling through, airports consuming. It's not a stockpile. It's a flow rate. The fuel exists because the system is running. Stop any part of the system, and that number starts dropping fast.

Every major industry runs this way now. Automotive plants keep two hours of parts on hand. Grocery stores restock shelves twice a week. Hospitals order supplies daily. The just-in-time revolution of the past forty years cut inventory levels across most sectors by 60-80%. That's not recklessness. That's math. Holding less inventory meant lower costs, faster adaptation, better returns. It worked.

Until it doesn't.

## The Trade-Off You Don't See

Here's the engineering problem at the heart of every supply chain: efficiency and resilience are opposites.

Every day of buffer inventory you add makes the system more expensive. You need more warehouses, more capital tied up, more staff managing stock, more risk of obsolescence. You're paying to insure against disruptions that might never come. For a business competing on price, that's a competitive disadvantage. Your rival who runs leaner can undercut you.

Every day of buffer you remove makes the system cheaper and faster—but more fragile. You save money every month. You respond to demand shifts in days instead of weeks. You outcompete the cautious players. Right up until a pipeline shuts down, a supplier fails, or a port closes. Then you have no cushion.

Companies optimize for normal times, not tail risks. That's not shortsightedness—it's a rational response to incentives. Shareholders reward quarterly efficiency. They don't reward paying for insurance that doesn't pay out. A CEO who maintains six months of inventory "just in case" gets replaced by one who runs leaner. The market selects for efficiency.

This is a feature of how modern capitalism works, not a bug. The system assumes continuity. It assumes suppliers deliver, logistics flow, demand stays predictable. And most of the time? Those assumptions hold. The system hums. Prices stay low. Shelves stay full. Just-in-time looks like genius.

Then something breaks.

A refinery goes offline. A hack shuts down a pipeline. A war disrupts shipping routes. Suddenly the efficiency becomes fragility. The system that looked robust because it never failed starts failing because it was never robust. Six weeks of fuel becomes four weeks, then two. And everyone realizes simultaneously that there's no backup plan because the backup plan was the system never breaking.

## Why One Break Becomes Many

The real danger isn't that one system runs low. It's that modern supply chains are interconnected.

If jets can't fly, cargo doesn't move. If cargo doesn't move, factories shut down. If factories shut down, they stop ordering supplies. If they stop ordering supplies, other suppliers start running out of customers. The ripple moves outward.

This is cascade failure. One node breaks, stress transfers to adjacent nodes, they buckle, stress spreads further. It's not linear—it's exponential. The first week of a fuel shortage inconveniences travelers. The third week grounds cargo flights. The fifth week shuts down industries dependent on air freight. By week six, you're not solving a fuel problem. You're solving a systemic problem.

Modern supply chains are built for efficiency, not redundancy. When something fails, there often isn't a backup supplier, an alternate route, or a reserve stock. The system ran lean everywhere. Everyone was just-in-time. Everyone assumed everyone else had buffers. Nobody did.

The 2021 semiconductor shortage showed this. One factory fire in Japan rippled through global auto production for eighteen months. Not because chips are rare—because every automaker had optimized inventory to near-zero. A two-week buffer became zero overnight. Assembly lines worldwide sat idle waiting for $2 chips to arrive. The cost of efficiency became visible.

Six weeks of jet fuel isn't a comfortable cushion. It's a countdown timer in a system with no redundancy.

## The Pattern Is Everywhere

This isn't unique to jet fuel or Europe.

Food supply chains run on days of inventory. Supermarkets restock constantly because holding more food means more spoilage, more cost, less flexibility. In normal times, this is why you can buy strawberries year-round. In abnormal times, this is why shelves empty in 48 hours.

The toilet paper panic of 2020 wasn't a shortage. Factories were producing the same amount. The problem was volatility. The system was optimized for steady demand—half residential, half commercial. When everyone stayed home, residential demand doubled overnight. Factories couldn't pivot fast enough. Stores couldn't restock fast enough. The pipeline was full, but it was flowing to the wrong places. A supply chain designed for predictability broke under unpredictability.

Medical supplies, construction materials, chemical inputs—same engineering problem. Keep inventory low to stay competitive. Assume steady supply to keep inventory low. Discover during crisis that everyone made the same assumption.

This is what "globalized supply chain" actually means in practice. Not just "made in many countries"—but "optimized for efficiency across many countries with no single point holding reserves." The benefits are enormous: lower costs, faster innovation, better products. The fragility is invisible until it's not.

## The Window

The warning isn't really about six weeks of fuel.

It's a window into how we've engineered modern life: hyper-efficient, hyper-connected, hyper-fragile. Understanding this doesn't make you panic—it makes you see the pattern everywhere, and why "just in time" keeps becoming "just too late."
