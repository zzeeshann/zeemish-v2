## hook

The Dow jumped 900 points today. The reason? Iran announced that during a ceasefire, the Strait of Hormuz would remain open to shipping. If you're wondering why a waterway you probably can't locate on a map just moved trillions of dollars in market value, you've identified the right question. The answer reveals something fundamental about how the modern world actually works: it runs through bottlenecks.

## what-is-hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz is a passage of water between Iran and Oman, connecting the Persian Gulf to the Gulf of Oman and then to the Arabian Sea. At its narrowest point, it's 21 miles wide. For scale: that's shorter than the distance from Manhattan to the Bronx. You could theoretically swim across it.

Through this narrow passage flows approximately 21% of the world's petroleum. That's roughly 21 million barrels per day. Add liquefied natural gas, and the energy figure climbs higher. Oil from Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, and Qatar—countries sitting on some of the planet's largest reserves—has exactly one way to reach global markets by sea: through the Strait of Hormuz.

The navigable channel is even narrower than the total width. The shipping lanes that supertankers actually use? About two miles wide in each direction. Separated by a two-mile buffer zone. The global economy's energy supply squeezes through a space you could jog across in twenty minutes.

## chokepoint-concept

Geographers and military strategists have a term for places like this: chokepoints. A chokepoint is any narrow passage that constrains flow. It's a place where geography forces concentration, where many routes funnel into one.

Chokepoints aren't just geographic features. They're leverage points. Control the chokepoint and you influence everything flowing through it. Close the chokepoint and you can halt systems far larger than yourself. This is why a regional conflict involving Iran—a country with a GDP smaller than Texas—can rattle global markets. Geography gave it a lever.

History is full of this pattern. Britain's empire ran on chokepoint control: Gibraltar (entrance to the Mediterranean), Suez (route to India), Singapore (gateway to China). The British didn't need to control every ocean. They controlled the narrow parts where ships had to pass. Whoever holds the hinge controls the door.

The Strait of Hormuz is the same principle at industrial scale. Iran doesn't need to sink ships to affect oil prices. The threat of closure is enough. Today's market rally wasn't celebrating peace—it was celebrating the restoration of flow through a critical node.

## other-chokepoints

Hormuz isn't unique. The global system runs through a handful of similar bottlenecks.

The Suez Canal connects the Mediterranean to the Red Sea. It handles roughly 12% of global trade. When the container ship Ever Given ran aground there in 2021, it blocked an estimated $9.6 billion worth of goods per day. The alternative route—around the southern tip of Africa—adds 3,500 miles and two weeks.

The Panama Canal links the Atlantic and Pacific without the trip around South America. About 6% of world trade passes through. When drought lowered water levels in 2023, the canal had to reduce daily ship transits from 36 to 24. Shipping costs spiked.

The Strait of Malacca, between Malaysia and Indonesia, carries about 25% of traded goods and one-third of global crude oil. Width at the narrowest: 1.7 miles. The detour through Indonesia's Lombok Strait adds days.

Bab el-Mandeb, the strait between Yemen and Djibouti, connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden. It's the southern gateway to the Suez Canal. When Houthi forces began attacking ships there in 2024, shipping companies rerouted around Africa, immediately affecting freight rates and delivery times.

The Taiwan Strait isn't just a geopolitical flashpoint. It's also a shipping lane between the East and South China Seas, carrying massive container traffic between Northeast Asian manufacturing and global markets.

But chokepoints aren't only maritime. Ninety-nine percent of intercontinental internet traffic travels through undersea fiber optic cables. These cables follow predictable routes and pass through identifiable points. Cut the right cables and you can isolate a continent.

Semiconductor manufacturing operates through an even tighter bottleneck. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company produces about 90% of the world's most advanced chips. ASML, a single Dutch company, makes the machines required to produce those chips. No ASML machine, no cutting-edge processors. The entire digital economy flows through two companies.

Rare earth element processing—critical for everything from smartphones to electric vehicles to wind turbines—is concentrated almost entirely in China. The U.S. and Europe mine some rare earths, but China controls approximately 90% of refining capacity. Geography of extraction matters less than geography of processing.

## why-chokepoints-persist

The obvious question: why not route around them? If chokepoints are vulnerabilities, why haven't we built alternatives?

Some reasons are purely physical. You can't dig a new Strait of Hormuz. The Panama and Suez canals exist because engineers could build them—but similar projects elsewhere would cost tens of billions and take decades. Russia has proposed developing the Northern Sea Route above Siberia as climate change melts Arctic ice, but it's navigable only part of the year and requires specialized ice-capable ships.

China's Belt and Road Initiative is partly an attempt to create overland alternatives to maritime chokepoints—rail and pipeline routes that bypass vulnerable straits. Pakistan's Gwadar Port is designed to give China access to the Arabian Sea without passing through Malacca. These are expensive, multi-decade infrastructure projects that still haven't replaced the chokepoints they're meant to bypass.

The deeper reason is path dependence. Global trade routes were established over centuries. Ports, refineries, pipelines, warehouses, and shipping schedules are all built around existing routes. Switching to an alternative isn't just about digging a new canal—it's about rebuilding the entire ecosystem of infrastructure that connects to the old route.

When the Ever Given blocked Suez, ships could have gone around Africa. Some did. But the route is longer, requires more fuel, takes more time, and doesn't end at the same ports. The supply chains on both ends of Suez are optimized for Suez. The alternative isn't just slower—it's incompatible with existing systems.

This is true for non-maritime chokepoints too. We know concentrating chip production in Taiwan creates risk, but building equivalent fabrication capacity elsewhere would cost hundreds of billions and take a decade. Meanwhile, every electronics company has suppliers, contracts, and logistics built around Taiwan's existing output. You can't just switch.

## fragility-insight

Here's the pattern underlying all of this: efficiency creates fragility. Global supply chains have spent decades optimizing for cost and speed. Just-in-time inventory. Minimal redundancy. Specialized suppliers. The fastest route through the fewest transfers.

Every optimization removes slack. Every removed buffer is one less thing that can absorb a shock. Chokepoints are a natural result of this process. Why use three shipping routes when one is cheapest? Why build redundant chip fabs when concentration enables economies of scale?

The system works beautifully until it doesn't. A canal blockage. A regional conflict. A drought. A pandemic. Suddenly the optimized system—designed for normal conditions—has no capacity to handle disruption. This is what markets remembered today when Iran announced the Strait would remain open. Not just that oil would flow, but that the system built around that flow could keep operating.

The alternative to chokepoints is redundancy. Multiple routes. Distributed production. Buffer inventory. Strategic reserves. All of which cost more and move slower than optimized systems. Companies won't build redundancy unless forced to—either by regulation or by experiencing enough disruptions that the cost of fragility exceeds the cost of resilience.

Some of this is changing. After 2021's supply chain shocks, companies started talking about "nearshoring" and "friend-shoring"—moving production closer to home or to allied countries. Chip fabs are being built in the U.S. and Europe, subsidized by governments worried about Taiwan dependence. But building resilience is expensive and slow. The chokepoints will persist for years, maybe decades.

Markets didn't rally today because of peace—they rallied because flow resumed through a critical node. The world economy runs through a handful of narrow passages: straits you could swim across, canals you could block with one ship, companies you could count on your fingers. Now you know where the hinges are.
