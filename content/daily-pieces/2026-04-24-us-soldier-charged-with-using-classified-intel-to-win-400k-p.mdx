## hook

A US soldier placed $400,000 in bets that Venezuela's president would be captured in a specific military raid. He won. Now he's charged with a federal crime. The bet itself wasn't illegal. Prediction markets—platforms where people wager on future events—operate openly. The classified information he accessed? Also not the crime, at least not directly. He had proper security clearance for his job.

So what did he actually do wrong? The answer reveals something fundamental about how markets work—and why they stop working when one person knows what everyone else is guessing at.

## prediction-markets-101

Prediction markets are betting platforms built on a specific theory: that crowds aggregate information better than experts. You pick an event—will it rain tomorrow, will a bill pass Congress, will a company's stock rise—and let people bet real money on the outcome. The theory says the price that emerges reflects the true probability better than any individual forecast could.

Here's the mechanism. Say a contract pays $1 if Maduro gets captured, $0 if he doesn't. If the market price settles at 30 cents, that suggests the crowd thinks there's a 30% chance of capture. People who think it's more likely buy contracts. People who think it's less likely sell. The price moves until it reaches equilibrium—the point where buyers and sellers agree on probability.

This only works under one condition: information is dispersed but relatively equal. Nobody knows everything, but everyone knows something. You might follow Venezuelan politics. I might understand military operations. Someone else might have sources in the region. We pool our partial knowledge through our bets. The price becomes a consensus estimate.

The soldier broke this. He didn't have partial knowledge. He had the operational plan. He knew the raid was happening, when, and how. His bet wasn't aggregating information with the crowd. It was extracting money from people who were genuinely uncertain while he had certainty. The market thought it was discovering truth. It was actually transferring wealth from the uninformed to the informed.

## information-asymmetry

Economists have a term for this: information asymmetry. It means one party in a transaction knows significantly more than the other. Not just "a bit more informed"—structurally, fundamentally more informed in a way the other party can't easily overcome.

George Akerlof won a Nobel Prize for describing what happens in these situations. His example: used car markets. Sellers know if their car is good or bad. Buyers don't. So buyers assume the worst and offer low prices. Good car owners won't sell at those prices, so they leave the market. Only bad cars remain. The market collapses. Economists call this adverse selection—when information imbalance drives out the participants who don't have the advantage.

Stock markets face this constantly. A corporate executive knows if the company is about to land a huge contract or lose a major lawsuit. If she can trade on that knowledge, she prints money. Everyone else is trading on public information—quarterly reports, analyst estimates, news articles. She's trading on the actual future. This isn't being "smart" or "well-informed." It's a structural advantage that can't be competed away.

That's why insider trading is illegal. Not because it's unfair in some abstract moral sense. Because it destroys the market's function. If insiders can trade freely, everyone else eventually realizes they're at a permanent disadvantage and stops participating. No liquidity, no price discovery, no market.

The soldier did insider trading, just in a prediction market instead of a stock market. He had material nonpublic information about a specific event. He traded on it. The people on the other side of his bets—the ones who sold him contracts at 30 cents that were actually worth $1—had no way to know they were betting against someone with the operational plans.

## adverse-selection

Here's what happens next if this behavior isn't stopped. Other people with classified access notice: prediction markets are exploitable. They start trading on secret information. Maybe not dramatically—small bets, careful timing. But word spreads within communities that have structural information advantages.

Regular participants start losing more than statistics say they should. They can't point to specific insider trades—they just notice their edge is gone. Some quit. Others get more cautious, which means less liquidity. The market starts to fail at its core job: aggregating dispersed public information.

This is adverse selection in action. The informed traders don't announce themselves. They just quietly extract value. The uninformed traders can't identify who has secret knowledge and who doesn't, so they start assuming everyone might. Trust collapses. Participation drops. Eventually you're left with a market where only insiders trade with each other, which defeats the entire purpose.

Financial regulators learned this the hard way. The 1920s stock market had minimal insider trading enforcement. Executives traded freely on private information. Small investors got crushed repeatedly. The 1929 crash and subsequent reforms weren't just about speculation—they were about rebuilding trust by ensuring material information was public before anyone could trade on it.

The same logic applies to prediction markets. If they become known as places where people with secret government information, or corporate insider knowledge, or advance access to data releases can profit, ordinary participants leave. The "wisdom of crowds" evaporates because the crowd realizes they're betting against people who aren't really forecasting—they're reading from an answer key.

## real-world-examples

This pattern repeats everywhere information asymmetry matters. Athletes can't bet on their own games. Not because they might throw matches—though that's a risk—but because they have nonpublic information about injuries, team dynamics, play-calling tendencies. A quarterback who knows the offensive line is dealing with undisclosed injuries has an edge no bettor can match.

Referees and umpires face even stricter rules. They don't just have information—they control outcomes. A ref who bets on games they officiate isn't even in information asymmetry territory anymore. They've moved to direct manipulation. But the principle is the same: you can't participate in a market where you have structural advantages others can't access.

Corporate executives deal with blackout periods and pre-clearance requirements for stock trades. Even when they're not trading on specific secrets, companies limit when executives can buy or sell because the mere possibility of insider knowledge poisons market confidence. The SEC requires public disclosure of executive trades precisely so other investors can watch for patterns.

Research institutions that compile economic data—employment numbers, housing starts, GDP estimates—have strict protocols about who can access information before public release and what they can do with it. A trader who sees the jobs report five minutes early would have a massive edge. So these organizations lock down pre-release data with physical security and legal consequences.

The soldier's case fits this pattern exactly. He had legitimate access to classified military information for his job. That access came with explicit restrictions: don't share the information, don't use it for personal benefit. Trading on prediction markets was using it for personal benefit. He converted his security clearance—a trust relationship—into a money-making scheme.

## close

The soldier's mistake was thinking prediction markets were just gambling. Put down money, take a chance, maybe get lucky. But prediction markets aren't casinos. They're information-gathering mechanisms that only work when participants are genuinely uncertain and contributing their best guesses. The $400,000 he won wasn't a prize for being smart. It was evidence that the market had broken—that someone had brought certainty into a system designed to aggregate uncertainty. Next time you see insider trading prosecutions, remember: they're not primarily about fairness. They're about preventing the knowledge gap that makes everyone else stop playing, which kills the market entirely.
