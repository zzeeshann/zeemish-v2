<beat id="hook">

Airlines can predict demand months ahead. They know how many passengers will fly through Denver next Tuesday. They optimize crew schedules down to the minute. But sometimes flights still get delayed because there isn't enough jet fuel at the airport. Not because of a global shortage. Because the fuel isn't *there*, at that airport, when the plane needs it. How does that happen in 2026?

The answer reveals something about nearly every system you depend on: food, medicine, semiconductors, hospital supplies. We've built a world that runs beautifully on normal days and wobbles dangerously when anything goes slightly wrong.

</beat>

<beat id="the-fuel-journey">

Jet fuel doesn't teleport. It starts as crude oil extracted from the ground. That crude travels by pipeline or tanker to a refinery—a specific refinery configured to process that type of crude into various products, including Jet A fuel. The refinery produces batches. Not continuously adjusted to this week's flight schedule, but in large production runs determined by crude availability, refinery maintenance schedules, and aggregate demand forecasts.

From the refinery, jet fuel moves through pipelines to regional terminals. These are large tank farms near major airports. The fuel sits in these tanks until airports need it. Then it moves again—sometimes by pipeline directly to the airport, sometimes by truck—into airport hydrant systems. These are underground pipe networks that connect to each gate position. When your plane pulls up, fuel flows through the hydrant into the aircraft.

Each step requires infrastructure: refinery capacity, pipeline throughput, storage tank volume, truck availability, hydrant system pressure. Each step requires timing: refineries schedule production weeks ahead, pipelines batch different products, storage facilities balance inflows and outflows, airports coordinate fueling with flight schedules. The fuel arrives when planes need it not by magic, but by coordinating all these steps with minimal slack.

</beat>

<beat id="just-in-time-vs-just-in-case">

There are two ways to run a supply chain. Just-in-time: keep inventory low, deliver exactly what's needed when it's needed. Just-in-case: keep buffer stock, maintain spare capacity, prepare for disruptions.

Just-in-time minimizes costs. Storage tanks cost money to build and maintain. Fuel sitting in tanks is capital tied up doing nothing. Excess refinery capacity means running below optimal production levels. Every bit of slack in the system is "waste" from an efficiency perspective. Airlines adopted just-in-time principles decades ago. So did their fuel suppliers. The whole chain optimized together.

Just-in-case costs more, visibly and immediately. You build larger storage facilities than normal demand requires. You maintain relationships with backup suppliers. You keep inventory "just sitting there." In good times, this looks like waste. Your competitor who cut these costs can offer lower prices. The market rewards efficiency.

But just-in-time has zero margin for error. There's no buffer between what's needed and what's available. The system works when every link performs exactly as planned. When one link weakens, there's nothing to absorb the gap.

</beat>

<beat id="why-it-breaks">

What weakens the links? Refinery maintenance. Refineries can't run continuously forever. They shut down for scheduled maintenance, sometimes for weeks. During that window, they produce zero jet fuel. If multiple refineries in a region schedule maintenance simultaneously, regional supply tightens. In a just-in-case system, tank farms would hold months of inventory to bridge the gap. In a just-in-time system, they hold weeks or days.

Pipeline capacity limits. Pipelines move multiple products—gasoline, diesel, jet fuel—in batches. If gasoline demand spikes (summer road trips), more pipeline capacity goes to gasoline. Less remains for jet fuel. You can't instantly add pipeline capacity. The fuel that doesn't flow today creates a shortage tomorrow.

Demand spikes. Holiday travel is predictable, but weather isn't. A winter storm reroutes hundreds of flights through alternative hubs. Those airports suddenly need 30% more fuel than forecast. In a system with slack, you tap reserves. Without slack, you ration. Some flights wait. Some get delayed. Some carry less fuel and make an extra stop.

Geopolitical shocks. A trade dispute affects crude oil imports. A regional conflict disrupts shipping routes. An export ban changes global flows. These create ripples. Refineries that counted on specific crude sources scramble for alternatives. Processing delays cascade downstream.

Each disruption is manageable in isolation, in a system with buffers. But we removed the buffers. So disruptions cascade. A refinery delay plus a pipeline batch timing issue plus a small demand spike equals airports without enough fuel.

</beat>

<beat id="beyond-jet-fuel">

This pattern appears everywhere. In early 2020, hospitals ran short of N95 masks. Not because humanity couldn't make masks. Because hospital supply chains kept minimal inventory. Masks arrived just-in-time for normal demand. Pandemic demand wasn't normal. There was no warehouse full of backup masks. The system had no slack.

In 2021, the global economy faced semiconductor shortages. Car manufacturers couldn't get chips. Not because chip factories stopped working. Because automakers had canceled orders during the pandemic, and chip foundries reallocated capacity to consumer electronics. When auto demand returned, there was no spare capacity to swing back. Chip fabrication plants take two years to build. You can't just-in-time a fab plant.

Food supply chains work the same way. Farms produce based on forward contracts. Processing plants run at optimized capacity. Distribution centers stock days, not months. A drought, a processing plant fire, a transportation strike—any single disruption tightens the whole chain. In 2022, baby formula shortages followed a single plant closure. One plant, serving a large percentage of the market, with no redundant capacity ready to absorb the gap.

The common thread isn't incompetence. It's optimization. Every system chose efficiency over resilience. They chose correctly, by the incentives they faced. Efficiency is profitable. Resilience is expensive. Until it isn't.

</beat>

<beat id="the-resilience-tax">

What would resilience actually cost? For jet fuel: more storage capacity at airports and regional terminals. Contracts with multiple refineries instead of the cheapest single supplier. Maintaining higher inventory levels even when demand is low. Pipeline capacity beyond peak forecasted need. 

For hospitals: warehouses full of PPE that might expire before use. Contracts guaranteeing supply even when there's no immediate need, which means paying suppliers to maintain spare capacity. Multiple sourcing for every critical item.

For semiconductors: spare fabrication capacity, idle unless needed. Multiple suppliers for each component. Inventory buffers at each stage from raw silicon to finished chips.

For food: additional processing plants running below capacity in normal times. Larger strategic reserves of staple goods. More redundant distribution routes.

All of this costs money. Tangible money, every quarter, visible on financial statements. The money shows up as higher costs, lower margins, capital tied up in "unproductive" assets. Shareholders see it. Competitors undercut you on price because they're not carrying those costs.

The benefit of resilience is invisible until crisis hits. Most years, nothing disrupts jet fuel supply. The extra storage capacity sits there, "wasted." Most years, there's no pandemic. The stockpiled masks expire and get replaced, pure expense with no return. Most years, chip demand is predictable. The spare fab capacity runs empty.

Resilience is insurance. You pay the premium hoping you never collect. But we've built systems where the premium is visible to everyone and the payout is invisible until disaster. So we collectively chose not to pay.

</beat>

<beat id="close">

Next time your flight is delayed for fuel, you're seeing a system working exactly as designed—optimized for normal days, fragile on abnormal ones.
