## hook

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. Through that passage, roughly 21 million barrels of oil move every day—about 20% of global petroleum supply. Iran can position military vessels in that strait, and headlines follow. But this isn't really about Iran. It's about chokepoints.

Why does a 21-mile-wide waterway command so much attention? Because when everything has to flow through one narrow passage, whoever controls that passage controls the flow. This pattern appears everywhere—in oceans, supply chains, internet infrastructure, your own work systems. Understanding chokepoints means understanding where power concentrates and why.

## what-is-chokepoint

A chokepoint is any place where geography forces flow to concentrate into a narrow passage. Ships, oil, data, goods—they all need to move from point A to point B. Sometimes there's a shortcut. Sometimes that shortcut is the only practical option. That's a chokepoint.

The Suez Canal is 120 miles long. It handles about 12% of global trade. Without it, ships traveling from Asia to Europe add roughly 6,000 miles by sailing around Africa. That's two extra weeks at sea, more fuel, higher insurance costs. So ships pay Egypt for passage instead. Egypt collects about $8 billion annually in transit fees. The alternative is simply too expensive.

The Panama Canal connects the Atlantic and Pacific. Ships avoid a 8,000-mile journey around South America's tip. About 6% of world trade moves through those locks. When drought lowered water levels in 2023, fewer ships could pass. Shipping costs spiked. No water, no transit. No transit, global commerce slows.

The Strait of Malacca sits between Indonesia and Malaysia. It's 1.5 miles wide at the narrowest point. About 25% of traded oil passes through. So does roughly one-third of global maritime trade. For ships moving between the Indian Ocean and the Pacific, the alternative route adds 1,000 miles. Most choose the strait.

These aren't random strategic points. They're places where geography leaves no good alternative. The shortcut is so much shorter that paying tolls, accepting delays, or risking political friction becomes the rational choice. Chokepoints exist because going around costs more than going through.

## leverage-and-cost

Controlling a chokepoint means you can impose costs on everyone who needs it. This creates asymmetric power—small geographic advantage, massive economic effect.

Consider the math for Hormuz. If Iran closed the strait for even one week, global oil supply would drop by about 147 million barrels. Alternative routes exist—pipelines across Saudi Arabia, longer shipping lanes around Africa—but they take time to activate and have limited capacity. Oil prices would spike immediately. Estimates suggest a sustained closure could add $20-40 per barrel. That's a global economic shock triggered by controlling 21 miles of water.

The chokepoint controller doesn't need to close the passage completely. Just making transit uncertain accomplishes the same goal. If ships face random inspections, delays, or military presence, insurance costs rise. Shippers reroute. Buyers diversify. The mere possibility of closure changes behavior across the entire system.

This leverage works because alternatives are expensive or slow. You can build a pipeline, but that takes years and billions of dollars. You can sail around Africa, but you burn more fuel and deliver late. You can stockpile reserves, but storage costs money and has limits. The chokepoint controller knows this. So do the users. That knowledge gap becomes negotiating power.

The same pattern appears outside maritime shipping. Three companies control about 90% of global shipping container capacity. One Dutch company, ASML, makes the lithography machines required for advanced semiconductor manufacturing. No other company produces equivalent technology. If ASML stopped selling, chip production would stall. That's a chokepoint in manufacturing technology.

Taiwan manufactures roughly 60% of the world's semiconductors and over 90% of the most advanced chips. Every smartphone, car, and data center depends on that production. Geography created this concentration—Taiwan invested heavily in foundries, developed expertise, and built supply relationships. Now the island is a chokepoint in the global electronics supply chain. Disruption there cascades everywhere.

Internet infrastructure has chokepoints too. About 95% of intercontinental data travels through undersea cables. These cables land at specific points on coastlines. Damage a cable, and data reroutes—if alternative paths exist. Some regions have redundancy. Others don't. The cable landing stations become control points for information flow.

Cloud computing concentrates in three providers: Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud. Together they control about 65% of the market. Most internet services run on these platforms. If one experiences an outage, millions of websites and apps go dark. This isn't inevitable—it's the result of economics favoring concentration. Building data centers requires massive capital. Once built, adding users costs little. Scale creates chokepoints.

## chokepoints-everywhere

Chokepoints aren't just about physical geography or global trade. They exist in every system where flow has to concentrate.

Supply chains have chokepoints at every level. A single factory might produce a critical component. One port might handle most imports for a region. One software library might underpin thousands of applications. The 2021 shortage of automotive semiconductors revealed this: a few factories in Asia produced the chips that nearly every car manufacturer needed. When COVID disrupted production, global auto manufacturing stopped. Not because cars are complicated—because the supply chain had a chokepoint.

Organizations have chokepoints too. One person who holds critical knowledge. One approval process that every decision must pass through. One system that every team depends on. When that person leaves, that process slows, or that system fails, work stops. The chokepoint wasn't visible until it closed.

Information has chokepoints. Academic publishing runs through a handful of companies. Visa and Mastercard process most electronic payments. App stores control software distribution on mobile devices. In each case, concentration creates control. The controller can set terms, charge fees, or restrict access. Users pay because the alternative—building a competing system—costs more than accepting the terms.

Understanding chokepoints changes how you see systems. When you map where flow concentrates, you find leverage points. Sometimes you want to control them. Sometimes you want to avoid them. Sometimes you want to build redundancy so a single chokepoint can't stop you.

Supply chain resilience is really about chokepoint management. If your business depends on one supplier, you have a chokepoint. If your product requires a component only one company makes, you have a chokepoint. If your data runs through one service provider, you have a chokepoint. Each represents a point where someone else's decision can stop your operation.

Reducing chokepoints costs money. Redundancy means paying for backup suppliers, alternative routes, spare capacity. That looks expensive—until the chokepoint closes. Then the cost of redundancy looks cheap compared to the cost of stoppage.

Some chokepoints are unavoidable. Geography doesn't change. Rare earth elements only exist in certain locations. Some technologies require massive scale to be economical. But many chokepoints exist because of decisions, not necessity. Concentration happens because it's efficient. Efficiency creates vulnerability.

This matters for personal decisions too. If your skills are only valuable to one employer, that's a chokepoint. If your income depends on one client, that's a chokepoint. If your work requires one tool or platform, that's a chokepoint. Identifying them doesn't mean you must eliminate them immediately. It means you know where your vulnerability lives.

## close

That 21-mile-wide strait isn't just about oil or Iran—it's about recognizing the pattern: where everything has to pass through, power concentrates.
