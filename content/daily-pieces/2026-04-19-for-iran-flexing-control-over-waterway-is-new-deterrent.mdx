## hook

Twenty-one miles wide. That's all it takes to hold 21% of the world's oil supply in check. The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow passage between Iran and Oman, is making headlines again — not because of a crisis, but because Iran is demonstrating what happens when you control the doorway everyone needs to walk through.

Why does a sliver of water barely wider than the distance between Manhattan and JFK Airport command so much attention from world powers?

## what-is-a-chokepoint

A chokepoint is a narrow passage where geography forces traffic into a single lane. Rivers, straits, canals, mountain passes — anywhere the world can't easily go around.

They matter because they concentrate power. When ships carrying oil, grain, or manufactured goods have no practical alternative route, whoever controls that passage controls the flow. They can charge tolls. They can threaten closure. They can simply make their presence known and watch insurance rates climb.

The Panama Canal connects the Atlantic and Pacific without the 8,000-mile detour around South America. The Suez Canal links Europe to Asia without circumnavigating Africa. The Strait of Malacca, between Malaysia and Indonesia, carries one-third of global maritime trade. Close any of these for a week and supply chains seize up globally.

Chokepoints are levers. Small pieces of geography that move large pieces of the world economy.

## the-hormuz-system

The Strait of Hormuz sits at the mouth of the Persian Gulf. On one side: Iran. On the other: Oman and the United Arab Emirates. Through the middle: roughly 21 million barrels of oil per day.

That's about 21% of global petroleum consumption. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, the UAE, and Qatar all export oil through Hormuz. There's no pipeline network that can replace that volume. There's no alternate sea route that doesn't add weeks and massive costs.

Here's the cascade if the strait closes: Insurance companies classify the route as high-risk. Premiums spike. Shipping companies pause or reroute. Oil that was priced at $80 per barrel jumps to $100, $120, higher depending on how long the closure lasts. Refineries pay more. Gas stations pay more. Airlines pay more. Plastics cost more. Fertilizer costs more.

Everything downstream from oil — which is nearly everything — gets more expensive.

This isn't speculation. In 2019, when tankers were attacked near Hormuz, Brent crude oil jumped 4% in a single day. The strait didn't close. Just the *possibility* moved markets.

That's the system. Hormuz is a 21-mile-wide valve on the global economy. Turn it even slightly and pressure changes everywhere.

## chokepoints-as-deterrent

Iran doesn't need to close the Strait of Hormuz to wield power over it. The threat is enough.

This is deterrence through geography. Traditional military deterrence works through capability: if you have missiles, troops, or nuclear weapons, adversaries calculate the cost of confronting you. Geographic deterrence is different. The asset is permanent. You can't sanction a strait. You can't negotiate it away.

Iran has explicitly stated it could block Hormuz if attacked. Whether it could maintain that blockade against naval opposition is debatable. What's not debatable: it could create chaos trying. Mine the channel. Sink a ship in the narrow lanes. Launch anti-ship missiles from the coast. The disruption alone achieves the goal.

Compare this to other chokepoints. Turkey controls the Bosphorus and Dardanelles, the straits connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean. This gives Turkey leverage over Russia's naval access and grain exports from Ukraine. Egypt controls the Suez Canal. In 2021, when the container ship *Ever Given* ran aground and blocked the canal for six days, it held up $9.6 billion in trade per day. Egypt didn't cause that accident, but it demonstrated the canal's value. Tolls from Suez generate roughly $9.4 billion annually for Egypt — a chokepoint turned into a revenue stream.

The pattern repeats: narrow passage, high dependence, concentrated power. The nation that controls the geography controls the negotiation.

## recognizing-the-pattern

You can learn to spot chokepoints on any map and predict their strategic importance. Three questions:

**Where does geography force a bottleneck?** Look for straits, canals, isthmuses, mountain passes. Places where terrain leaves only one practical route. The Strait of Gibraltar. The Bab-el-Mandeb between Yemen and Djibouti. The Khyber Pass between Afghanistan and Pakistan.

**Who depends on it?** Trace the traffic. If a chokepoint carries oil, ask which economies need that oil. If it carries container ships, ask which factories depend on those goods. If it's a land route, ask which populations or militaries need access. High dependence equals high leverage.

**What happens if it closes?** Run the scenario. Can traffic reroute? At what cost? How long would the alternative take? If rerouting adds weeks or doubles costs, the chokepoint is critical. If there's an easy workaround, it's not.

Apply this to Hormuz. Geography: a strait 21 miles wide, the only deepwater exit from the Persian Gulf. Dependence: six major oil exporters, 21% of global supply. Closure cost: no viable alternative at scale, prices spike globally within hours.

Apply it to Taiwan. Geography: an island 100 miles from mainland China, sitting astride shipping lanes that carry goods from East Asia to the world. Dependence: semiconductor manufacturing (Taiwan produces over 60% of global chips), plus shipping routes for Japan and South Korea. Closure cost: supply chain collapse for electronics, automotive, and countless other industries.

Chokepoints aren't just about water. They're about control points in systems. Identify the bottleneck, map the dependencies, and you understand the leverage.

## close

The Strait of Hormuz is one example of a universal pattern: geography creates power, and chokepoints turn maps into negotiations.
