<lesson-beat type="hook">

Airlines are scrambling. Jet fuel inventories have dropped below comfortable levels, and carriers are adjusting routes, raising prices, and watching their margins shrink. But this isn't a fuel shortage. Refineries are running. Oil is available. The problem is that nobody kept much extra fuel sitting around in the first place.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat type="teaching">

Why don't airlines—or fuel suppliers—just stockpile more? The answer is a deliberate choice that shapes every product you buy, every service you use, and every delay you've ever experienced. The choice between efficiency and resilience. Between running minimal inventory and keeping reserves. Between "just enough" and "more than we need."

The system causing today's fuel headache has a name: Just-in-Time inventory. Instead of warehousing months of supplies, you order precisely what you need, precisely when you need it. You run with minimal stock—stock so low that you measure it in days or hours, not months.

Toyota created this in the 1970s. American car factories kept enormous stockpiles—engine blocks in warehouses, seats gathering dust, transmissions in inventory for weeks. All that stock tied up cash and required storage space. Some parts became obsolete before they were ever installed.

Toyota flipped it. They coordinated with suppliers for same-day delivery, not months-ahead stockpiling. Inventory shrank from weeks to hours. The savings: 20-30% cost reduction in most cases. Less warehouse space, less capital locked in unused parts, less waste from obsolete components.

The idea replicated everywhere. Your grocery store doesn't warehouse three months of milk—trucks deliver fresh stock every few days. Hospitals don't stockpile a year's worth of surgical masks. Amazon built an empire on moving products *through* warehouses, not storing them *in* warehouses.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat type="teaching">

Every inventory system runs on a spectrum. On one end: operations that minimize waste and maximize cash flow. On the other: buffered systems with extra stock, backup suppliers, and redundancy built in.

Run with minimal inventory and you cut costs dramatically. But you become vulnerable. A single supplier hiccup—a storm, a strike, a surprise spike in demand—and you have no cushion.

Build in buffers and you gain resilience. When disruptions hit, you have reserves. But you pay in storage costs, tied-up capital, expired products.

The math: if disruptions are rare, minimal inventory wins. You save money 95% of the time and eat the cost of scrambling during the occasional crisis. If disruptions become frequent, buffers start looking smart.

The jet fuel situation shows what happens when the bet fails. Refineries scheduled routine maintenance. Demand spiked 5% higher than predicted. Normally, suppliers would dip into reserves to smooth out the fluctuation. But when you're running Just-in-Time, there *are* no reserves. A 5% unexpected increase creates a genuine shortage, even though total supply capacity is fine.

Tight fuel supplies mean higher prices. Higher fuel costs mean airlines raise ticket prices or cut marginal routes. Airports with less traffic see restaurants and shops lose revenue. Connecting flights get rescheduled, creating ripples across the network.

This cascade repeats across industries. In 2021, a single semiconductor factory in Japan caught fire. The global auto industry lost millions of vehicles in production because no one kept extra chips in inventory. One factory fire—minimal direct damage—caused a worldwide shortage lasting months.

March 2020: hospitals didn't stockpile N95 masks because masks expire, storage costs money, and pandemics are rare. When COVID hit, the entire healthcare system discovered simultaneously that there was no buffer. Not just insufficient—genuinely none.

Grocery stores in 2020 showed this. Toilet paper didn't actually run out—factories kept producing at normal rates. But grocery stores only kept a few days of inventory on hand. When demand jumped 20% and stayed there, shelves emptied. The system couldn't flex because it was optimized for efficiency, not resilience.

Each case follows the same logic. Someone calculated: disruptions are rare enough that carrying extra inventory costs more than occasionally scrambling. That calculation was correct—until it wasn't.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat type="teaching">

After COVID, chip shortages, and now fuel supply squeezes, companies are recalculating.

Some industries are moving toward buffered systems. Apple now keeps 6-8 weeks of component inventory instead of 2-3. That's expensive—billions of dollars sitting in warehouses—but it buys protection. Medical supply chains are building strategic reserves of critical items. The federal government is subsidizing domestic semiconductor production to create backup capacity.

Other sectors are doubling down on Just-in-Time. Airlines are staying the course, betting that fuel supply disruptions remain rare enough that paying for extra storage capacity doesn't make sense. Grocery stores are sticking with daily deliveries because COVID panic-buying was an outlier.

Both are bets about how often the system breaks. Disruptions are becoming more frequent—climate events increase 15% year-over-year, geopolitical instability is measurable in trade route disruptions, infrastructure ages predictably. Or recent years are statistical noise and things revert to normal.

The companies adding buffers are paying insurance premiums every day. The companies staying with minimal inventory are accepting risk in exchange for lower costs. Different wagers about how the world will behave.

What's changed is that the choice is now conscious. For years, Just-in-Time won by default because it worked. Now executives actively debate the tradeoff. How many days of inventory? How many backup suppliers? How much redundancy?

</lesson-beat>

Someone decided how much buffer was enough. Now you're finding out if they were right.
