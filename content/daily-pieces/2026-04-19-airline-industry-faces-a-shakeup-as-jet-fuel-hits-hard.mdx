## hook

Axios reports airlines are merging and cutting routes as jet fuel prices spike. United and Delta are abandoning secondary markets. Regional carriers are grounding planes. Industry analysts predict further consolidation by year-end.

Here's the puzzle: jet fuel represents only 20-25% of an airline's operating costs. So why does a 20% increase in fuel prices create an existential crisis? Why do routes that were profitable last quarter suddenly vanish? The answer reveals a fundamental economic fragility hiding in plain sight—in airlines, yes, but also in restaurants, trucking companies, manufacturers, and probably a business near you.

## teaching-1-fixed-vs-variable

Airlines operate under a brutal cost structure. Before a single passenger boards, the airline has already committed to massive expenses: the aircraft lease or loan payment, gate fees at airports, crew salaries, insurance, maintenance schedules, IT systems, corporate overhead. These are fixed costs—they don't change whether the plane flies full, half-empty, or completely empty.

Fuel is different. Fuel is variable. You only burn it when you fly. And critically, fuel is the ONLY major variable cost airlines can control flight-by-flight. Labor contracts lock in crew costs. Airports charge the same gate fees regardless. The plane payment is the plane payment.

Let's use simple numbers to see why this matters. A single flight might cost $50,000 in fixed costs—the plane, crew, gate, systems. Add $20,000 in fuel. Total: $70,000. If the flight brings in $200,000 in ticket revenue, the airline profits $130,000.

Now fuel prices spike 50%. That $20,000 becomes $30,000. Your new profit: $120,000. You lost $10,000 in profit, but you're still deeply profitable. Not catastrophic, right?

Wrong. Because that math assumes ticket prices stay constant and passengers keep buying. In reality, airlines face fierce competition. If United raises prices to cover fuel costs, Southwest might not. Passengers switch. Load factors drop. That $200,000 in revenue starts falling to $180,000, then $160,000.

Suddenly, a route that cleared $130,000 per flight is clearing $40,000. Or $20,000. Or losing money. The entire profit margin exists in that narrow space between fixed costs and revenue—and fuel just ate most of it.

## teaching-2-operating-leverage

This is operating leverage, and it works like a seesaw with a brick wall on one end. High fixed costs create enormous sensitivity to changes in either volume or variable costs. Small movements get amplified into massive profit swings.

When fuel costs $20,000 per flight, a route generating $200,000 in revenue produces $130,000 in profit. That's a 65% profit margin. Excellent. But when fuel hits $30,000 and competitive pressure drops revenue to $170,000, profit falls to $90,000. You just lost 31% of your profit from a 15% revenue decline and a 50% fuel increase.

This is why airlines can't simply absorb fuel shocks. The cost structure doesn't allow it. They can't quickly shed fixed costs—planes are under multi-year leases, labor contracts run for years, airport gates are locked in. The only available levers are: raise prices (lose customers to competitors), cut routes (abandon markets and revenue), or merge with competitors (reduce competition so pricing power increases).

Fuel shocks trigger consolidation waves not because airlines are poorly managed, but because operating leverage makes them structurally fragile. Fewer competitors means more pricing power. More pricing power means the ability to pass fuel costs to customers without losing volume. Consolidation is survival strategy.

Now zoom out. Restaurants have the same structure. Rent is fixed. Labor is mostly fixed (you can't run a restaurant with half a kitchen staff). Food costs are variable, but only 28-35% of revenue. A 20% spike in food costs—say, beef or cooking oil prices jump—can eliminate the entire profit margin on certain menu items. The restaurant can't easily move locations or renegotiate the lease. So they raise menu prices (lose customers), cut menu items (lose variety and revenue), or close (exit the market). Same operating leverage, different industry.

Trucking companies: trucks, insurance, and driver salaries are fixed. Diesel is variable, but represents 30-40% of costs. A diesel spike forces rate increases, route cuts, or company failures. Manufacturing: factory costs, equipment, and baseline labor are fixed. Raw materials are variable. A steel or semiconductor shortage doesn't just raise costs—it can make entire product lines unprofitable overnight.

## teaching-3-why-it-matters

Operating leverage is the invisible vulnerability map of the economy. It tells you which businesses can absorb shocks and which ones shatter.

Low fixed costs, high variable costs: flexible and resilient. Consulting firms pay for office space and some salaries, but most costs scale with projects—hire consultants when needed, scale back when demand drops. Commodity price shocks barely register. Software companies have even better ratios: once the product is built, adding customers costs almost nothing. A cloud hosting bill might rise, but it's trivial compared to revenue. These businesses bend under pressure.

High fixed costs, low variable costs: rigid and fragile. Airlines, restaurants, manufacturers, commercial real estate, hospitals, universities. These businesses need volume and stable input costs to survive. When variable costs spike or volume drops, the fixed cost base becomes an anchor. They can't flex. They either pass costs to customers (if they have pricing power) or they fail.

This explains consolidation patterns across industries. When you see a commodity shock—fuel, steel, food ingredients—followed by waves of mergers and closures, you're watching operating leverage in action. The businesses with the highest fixed-cost ratios die first or merge to gain pricing power. The survivors aren't necessarily better operators. They just have cost structures that can absorb the shock, or enough market share to dictate prices.

It also explains why some local businesses survive recessions while others close immediately. The bakery with owned property and equipment (low fixed costs relative to revenue) can ride out a flour price spike. The bakery with a high-rent lease and new equipment loans (high fixed costs) cannot. Same business, same product, different cost structure, different outcome.

## close

Those airline mergers and route cuts aren't random responses to fuel prices—they're the predictable outcome of operating leverage meeting a variable cost shock. Next time you see an industry in crisis over "rising costs," ask: what's fixed, what's variable, and what's the ratio? That ratio is the fragility map. The airlines are just showing us the formula in dramatic, visible form.
