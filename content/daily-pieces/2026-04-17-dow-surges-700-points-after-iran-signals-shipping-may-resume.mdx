## hook

The Dow surged 700 points yesterday. Not because of an earnings report, a tech breakthrough, or a policy announcement — because Iran signaled that shipping might resume through the Strait of Hormuz. A waterway most Americans couldn't find on a map moved markets more than most corporate news ever will.

Why does a narrow strip of ocean between Iran and Oman control Wall Street's mood?

## what-is-a-chokepoint

A chokepoint is where geography forces global trade through a narrow passage with no easy alternative.

The Strait of Hormuz is 21 miles wide at its narrowest point. It connects the Persian Gulf — where oil tankers load up at Saudi, Emirati, Kuwaiti, and Iraqi ports — to the Gulf of Oman and then the open Indian Ocean. Every drop of oil leaving the Persian Gulf passes through this strait. There is no other practical route.

That's 21 million barrels per day in normal times. Roughly 21% of global petroleum consumption. The numbers align by coincidence, but the meaning doesn't: one-fifth of the world's oil supply depends on safe passage through a channel narrower than the distance between Manhattan and Newark.

When that passage is threatened, prices spike. When it reopens, markets celebrate. The physical bottleneck creates an economic bottleneck. Control the geography, control the leverage.

## why-chokepoints-matter

Narrow waterways have outsized power for three reasons.

First: trade concentration. Supertankers can't take detours. A fully loaded Very Large Crude Carrier weighs 200,000 tons and draws 20 meters of water. You can't reroute it through shallow coastal waters or overland pipelines that don't exist. The ship goes where deep water goes — and deep water often means chokepoints.

Second: closure risk. Even temporary blockage causes price spikes. In March 2021, the container ship Ever Given got wedged sideways in the Suez Canal. The canal was blocked for six days. Global trade lost an estimated $9–10 billion. Oil prices jumped. Shortages rippled through supply chains for months. That was an accident. Imagine a deliberate closure.

Third: military vulnerability. It's easier to threaten a narrow passage than a vast ocean. You don't need a massive navy to close a chokepoint — you need mines, missiles, or the credible threat of using them. Defending a chokepoint requires constant presence. Threatening one requires occasional posturing.

This isn't new. The British Empire obsessed over the Suez Canal and the Strait of Gibraltar. The Ottoman Empire controlled the Turkish Straits and extracted tolls. The strategic value of narrow water has shaped empires for centuries.

## the-hormuz-system

Hormuz isn't just any chokepoint — it's the chokepoint for global energy.

The Persian Gulf holds about 48% of the world's proven oil reserves and 38% of natural gas reserves. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar — they all export through Hormuz. There is no land route that can replace that volume. Pipelines exist, but they carry a fraction of what tankers do.

When Iran threatens to close the strait, oil futures spike immediately. Not because supply actually stops — because markets price in risk. Traders know that even a temporary disruption would create shortages in Asia (where most Persian Gulf oil goes), spike prices globally, and cascade through every industry that relies on fuel or petrochemical feedstocks.

Today's news reverses that risk premium. Iran signals the waterway stays open. Markets exhale. The Dow jumps 700 points — not because anything fundamentally improved in the economy, but because a major tail risk decreased.

This is the hidden power of geography. A 21-mile-wide strait has more influence over global oil prices than most OPEC meetings.

## other-chokepoints

Hormuz isn't alone. The world economy depends on a handful of narrow passages, and each one is a potential crisis point.

The Suez Canal connects the Mediterranean to the Red Sea. About 12% of global trade volume passes through it — container ships from Asia to Europe, oil tankers heading north. The alternative route around Africa's Cape of Good Hope adds 3,500 miles and weeks of transit time. When the Ever Given blocked the canal in 2021, the world remembered how fragile this shortcut is.

The Strait of Malacca sits between Malaysia and Indonesia, connecting the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea. It's the shortest route between the Middle East and East Asia. About 25% of traded oil passes through it, plus enormous container traffic. It's also narrow — 1.5 miles at its tightest — and plagued by piracy. Singapore's prosperity is built partly on sitting at the strait's eastern exit.

The Panama Canal cuts 8,000 miles off the journey between the Atlantic and Pacific. When drought lowers water levels, the canal restricts traffic — which happened in 2023, delaying shipments and raising costs.

The Bab el-Mandeb Strait connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden, funneling shipping between the Suez Canal and the Indian Ocean. It's 18 miles wide, bordered by Yemen (unstable) and Eritrea and Djibouti (where multiple foreign militaries have bases for exactly this reason).

The Turkish Straits — the Bosphorus and Dardanelles — are the only route between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean. Russia's Black Sea Fleet must pass through them to reach the open ocean. Turkey controls access. That's why Turkey, despite being smaller than most of its neighbors, has outsized geopolitical weight.

Each of these waterways is a geographic lever. This is why nations fight over seemingly insignificant strips of water — they're economic arteries, not just lines on a map.

## fragility-of-globalization

Globalization works because shipping is cheap, predictable, and secure.

You can manufacture a product in Vietnam, ship components from Taiwan and Germany, assemble in Mexico, and sell in Ohio — all for less than making everything in one place. But that system depends on ships moving freely through established routes. Those routes, in turn, depend on chokepoints staying open.

When one closes, there's rarely a Plan B. Alternatives exist in theory — reroute around Africa instead of through Suez, ship overland instead of by sea — but they're slower and vastly more expensive. Modern supply chains are optimized for the current map. Change the map, and the whole system wobbles.

This is the hidden architecture beneath "just-in-time" manufacturing, two-day shipping, and $50 consumer electronics. It assumes the Strait of Malacca stays open, the Suez Canal stays clear, and Hormuz keeps flowing.

That assumption is usually correct — which is why we forget it's an assumption. Then something happens: a ship gets stuck, a country makes a threat, a canal runs low on water. Prices spike. Shortages appear. Markets panic.

Today's 700-point Dow surge is the mirror image of that panic. The assumed stability returns. The geographic bottleneck stays open. Trade flows resume.

## close

The Dow didn't rise because the economy grew or innovation accelerated — it rose because a waterway stayed open.
