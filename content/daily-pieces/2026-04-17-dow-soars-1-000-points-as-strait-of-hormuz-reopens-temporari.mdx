## hook

The Dow soared 1,000 points yesterday. The reason: a 21-mile-wide strip of water between Iran and Oman reopened to tanker traffic after a two-week closure.

How does a narrow waterway halfway around the world control stock prices in New York? The answer reveals something fundamental about how the modern world actually functions.

## what-is-hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz sits between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman. At its narrowest point, it's 21 miles wide.

Through this passage flows roughly 21 million barrels of oil per day—about 21% of global petroleum consumption. Add natural gas, and you're looking at nearly a third of the world's seaborne energy trade.

There's no alternative route. Ships leaving Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Iraq, the UAE, and Qatar must pass through Hormuz to reach the Indian Ocean and beyond. The only other option is pipelines that can't handle the volume, or trucking overland through war zones and mountain ranges.

When Hormuz closes, that oil doesn't go somewhere else. It just stops moving. Refineries in Asia start calculating how many days of reserves they have. European governments check their strategic petroleum reserves. Traders price in scarcity.

When it reopens, markets exhale.

## chokepoint-concept

Hormuz is what geographers call a chokepoint: a narrow passage that controls access to a much larger area.

Geography creates bottlenecks. Trade routes develop through them because they're shortest. Supply chains depend on them staying open.

This isn't an oil problem or a Middle East problem—it's structural. The world has perhaps a dozen critical maritime chokepoints. Each one follows the same logic.

Whoever controls the chokepoint has leverage over everyone who needs to use it. And everyone who depends on it is vulnerable.

## other-chokepoints

The Suez Canal connects the Mediterranean to the Red Sea. Without it, ships traveling between Europe and Asia would sail around the entire continent of Africa—adding 3,500 miles and two weeks. About 12% of global trade by volume passes through Suez.

The Panama Canal cuts 8,000 miles off the trip from New York to San Francisco. Roughly 6% of global maritime commerce crosses through Panama's locks.

The Strait of Malacca, between Malaysia and Indonesia, handles even more traffic. A quarter of the world's traded goods pass through it, including 80% of China's crude oil imports. The strait is 1.5 miles wide at its narrowest point.

The Turkish Straits—the Bosporus and Dardanelles—connect the Black Sea to the Mediterranean. Russian and Ukrainian grain, plus petroleum from Central Asia, all funnel through here to reach global markets. The Bosporus runs through Istanbul. It's 2,300 feet wide at one point.

Each of these passages handles throughput that seems impossible for their size. Each has no viable alternative.

## why-vulnerable

You can't reroute a supply chain the way you reroute internet traffic.

Ships are physical objects weighing hundreds of thousands of tons. Canals and straits have fixed locations. Ports, refineries, factories, and distribution centers are built around predictable shipping routes.

When a chokepoint closes, the alternatives are brutal. Sailing around Africa instead of through Suez adds $300,000 in fuel costs per voyage. Ships arrive two weeks late. Contracts breach. Production stops. Inventory runs out.

The economics get worse at scale. When hundreds of ships reroute simultaneously, port capacity overloads. Fuel prices spike from sudden demand. Insurance rates jump. Some ships physically can't take the alternative route because they're too large for different sea conditions.

This creates strategic value. Control a chokepoint and you can extract fees, set terms, or close it. Every user must either accept your conditions or absorb massive costs.

It also creates fragility. One accident, one military decision, one political crisis—and a system designed for uninterrupted flow stops working.

## historical-pattern

Britain spent three centuries controlling Gibraltar, Suez, Aden, Singapore, and the Cape of Good Hope. These weren't random colonial outposts. They were the critical nodes in global shipping routes. Control them, and you controlled world trade.

The Dardanelles has triggered wars since ancient times. Ottoman control of the strait gave them leverage over everyone trading between the Mediterranean and Black Sea. During World War I, the Allies launched the disastrous Gallipoli campaign specifically to open that passage.

Russia's strategic problem for 300 years has been access to warm-water ports. Every exit from the Black Sea or Baltic must pass through chokepoints controlled by other nations. This shapes Russian foreign policy to this day.

The pattern holds: trade flows through chokepoints, chokepoints become points of conflict, and whoever controls them has power disproportionate to the size of the territory.

Modern warfare reflects this. Nations don't need to invade trading partners to disrupt their economy. They just need to threaten a chokepoint. One credible threat to close Hormuz and oil futures spike 20%.

## modern-complications

Today's global economy amplifies the vulnerability.

Modern logistics runs on "just-in-time" principles. Factories order precisely what they need, timed to arrive exactly when production requires it. This eliminates waste and storage costs. It also eliminates buffers.

The 2021 Suez Canal blockage demonstrated this. A single container ship—the Ever Given—got stuck sideways in the canal. Six days. Furniture delayed for months because component parts couldn't arrive. Car manufacturers shut down assembly lines. Over 400 ships queued at both ends. The backlog took weeks to clear.

Total estimated cost: $10 billion per day of disrupted trade.

One ship. One canal. Six days.

The Hormuz closure was worse. Two weeks of stopped flow affects not just goods in transit, but production decisions, contract negotiations, futures markets, and national energy policy.

The problem compounds because supply chains are interconnected. A chip manufacturer in Taiwan depends on chemicals from Germany, which depend on shipping through Malacca. A delay at one chokepoint cascades through the network.
