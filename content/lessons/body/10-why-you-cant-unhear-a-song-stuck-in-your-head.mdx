<lesson-beat name="hook">

Right now, somewhere in your brain, a song is playing. You didn't ask for it. You can't stop it. And the harder you try, the louder it gets.

This isn't a memory problem. You're not choosing to recall the song. Your brain is replaying it on its own — a loop you can hear but can't control. The melody cycles through the same sixteen bars. The chorus lands on the same notes. And when you notice it's happening, when you think "Stop thinking about this song," the volume goes up.

This is an earworm. The involuntary musical replay that 98% of people experience regularly. It feels like the song is stuck, but what's actually stuck is the mechanism your brain uses to process sound. And the reason you can't turn it off is the same reason you can't ignore an instruction not to think about a white bear: your brain can't monitor for something without activating it.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-1">

## Your brain has a sound rehearsal system that won't quit

Your working memory — the cognitive workspace where you hold information temporarily — contains a component called the phonological loop. This is the part of your brain that rehearses sound. When someone gives you a phone number and you repeat it silently until you can write it down, you're using the phonological loop. It holds auditory information by replaying it.

The loop is automatic. You don't consciously decide to activate it. When sound enters your auditory cortex, the phonological loop engages to process and store it. For speech, this makes sense. For music, it creates a problem.

Music has patterns. Verse, chorus, verse, chorus, bridge. Melodies that rise and fall in predictable intervals. Rhythms that repeat. Your brain is a pattern-completion machine, and the phonological loop treats these musical patterns the same way it treats any other sound sequence: it rehearses them to maintain them in working memory.

But songs don't resolve the way phone numbers do. A phone number has a clear end — seven or ten digits, then you're done. A song has structural repetition. The chorus comes back. The hook repeats. Even after the song ends, your brain hasn't received a clear signal that the pattern is complete. So the phonological loop keeps rehearsing.

James Kellaris, a marketing professor who studied earworms for over a decade, found that 98% of people experience involuntary musical imagery. The songs most likely to stick share specific traits: they're simple, they're repetitive, and they have what musicians call "high melodic contour" — lots of ups and downs in pitch that create a memorable shape.

Pop songs are engineered for this. The chorus of "Call Me Maybe" repeats the same melodic phrase four times in a row. "We Will Rock You" is two drum hits and a stomp, over and over. These songs hijack the phonological loop because they're built from the exact kind of repetitive pattern your brain wants to rehearse.

You're not remembering the song. Your auditory system is actively generating it, the same way it generates the silent voice you hear when you read these words. The song is playing in your head because your phonological loop is playing it.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-2">

## Trying not to think about it is thinking about it

In 1987, psychologist Daniel Wegner ran an experiment. He told participants not to think about a white bear for five minutes. Whenever the thought occurred to them, they should ring a bell. Then he had them spend the next five minutes thinking about a white bear as much as they wanted, ringing the bell each time.

The group that tried to suppress the thought first had a rebound effect. During the second phase, they thought about the white bear more frequently than the control group — the people who hadn't been told to suppress anything.

Wegner called this ironic process theory. When you try not to think about something, your brain runs two simultaneous processes. One is the operating process: the intentional attempt to focus on something else. The other is the monitoring process: the unconscious system that checks whether you're succeeding.

The monitor has to search for the forbidden thought to know you're avoiding it. "Am I thinking about the white bear? No. Good." But that search requires accessing the representation of the white bear. The monitor generates the very thought it's trying to detect.

This is why trying to stop thinking about a song makes it worse. Every time you notice the song is playing and think "I need to stop this," you're running a check. Your brain searches for the song to see if it's still there. The search activates the phonological loop. The loop starts rehearsing again.

You can't unhear the song because listening for silence requires listening. The act of monitoring for the absence of music creates the musical replay. Your brain doesn't have a "not this" instruction — only a "check for this" function. And checking is activating.

The paradox is complete: the song plays because you're trying to stop it. Your intention to silence the loop is what keeps it running.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-3">

## The loop wants an ending

The phonological loop isn't broken when a song gets stuck. It's doing exactly what it's designed to do: rehearse a sound pattern until the pattern resolves. The problem is that the pattern hasn't resolved. Your brain heard part of a song, or looped through the chorus a few times, and now the rehearsal system is waiting for completion.

This is why distraction doesn't work. You can focus on something else — read, work, talk to someone — and the song keeps playing underneath. You're not thinking about the song consciously, but the phonological loop isn't conscious. It's a lower-level auditory process that runs in parallel with your attention. Shifting your attention away doesn't shut it down.

What works is completion. If the loop wants to finish the pattern, let it finish.

Studies on earworm resolution show that listening to the stuck song all the way through — start to finish, including the ending — stops the replay. The phonological loop gets the closure it was searching for. The pattern completes. The rehearsal ends.

If you don't want to listen to the whole song, you can replace it with a different song that has a strong ending. The new song occupies the phonological loop and provides a resolution point. This works better than random distraction because you're not fighting the loop — you're giving it something else to complete.

Ira Hyman at Western Washington University found that cognitive load helps too, but only if the task uses the same verbal rehearsal system. Anagrams work. Sudoku doesn't. The phonological loop needs to be busy with something linguistic or musical. If it has spare capacity, it goes back to rehearsing the song.

The strangest intervention is chewing gum. A 2010 study by Philip Beaman and Tom Williams showed that chewing gum significantly reduced musical imagery. The mechanism: your phonological loop uses the same motor system for subvocal rehearsal (the silent "voice" in your head) that you use for chewing. When your jaw is busy chewing, it can't rehearse the song. The loop can't generate the phantom sound of the melody because the motor system is occupied.

This explains why you don't get earworms while eating or talking. The phonological loop isn't available. It's already in use.

The song in your head isn't a memory you keep accidentally recalling. It's an active process — a loop that's running because it started something it hasn't finished. You can't stop it by trying not to think about it. You can only resolve it by letting the pattern complete, or by occupying the system with something that closes the loop.
