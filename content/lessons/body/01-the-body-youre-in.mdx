<lesson-beat name="hook">

You've had a body your whole life. You've never read the manual.

That's not your fault. There isn't one. What exists instead is a pile of contradictory advice, most of it trying to sell you something. Drink more water. No, not that much. Stretch every morning. Actually, stretching doesn't do what you think. Sleep eight hours. Unless you're one of the people who need seven. Or nine.

This course isn't the manual either. But it's closer than most things you'll find.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-1">

## What your body actually is

Here's something nobody tells you in school: your body is not a machine. That's a metaphor, and it breaks down fast.

Machines have parts that wear out and get replaced. Your body has parts that get stronger when you use them and weaker when you don't. Your bones get denser when you put weight on them. Your muscles grow when you tear them. Your brain builds new connections when you make it do hard things.

This is called adaptation. It's the single most important thing about your body, and it changes how you should think about everything from exercise to stress to sleep.

A machine breaks down over time. A body that's used well builds up over time. Not forever — entropy wins eventually — but for longer than most people expect.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-2">

## The two systems you're running

Right now, as you read this, two branches of your nervous system are doing different jobs.

The **sympathetic** branch is your accelerator. It speeds up your heart, sharpens your focus, sends blood to your muscles. It's the system that fires when something surprises you or when you need to perform.

The **parasympathetic** branch is your brake. It slows your heart, relaxes your muscles, diverts energy to digestion and repair. It's the system that runs when you're safe and calm.

You need both. The problem isn't having an accelerator — it's having one that's stuck on. Modern life presses the accelerator constantly: notifications, deadlines, arguments, screens, noise. The brake rarely gets its turn.

Most of what you'll learn in this course is about giving the brake its turn. Not because calm is the goal, but because a system that can shift between fast and slow is healthier than one that's stuck in either gear.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="practice">

## Try this now

Sit however you're sitting. Don't adjust anything.

Notice your breathing. Not to change it — just to notice. Is it fast or slow? Shallow or deep? Through your nose or your mouth?

You don't need to do anything with this information. The noticing is the practice. You'll find out why in the next lesson.

</lesson-beat>

Your body has been running itself without your input for decades. That's fine. But there's a difference between a body that runs on autopilot and one whose owner occasionally checks the instruments.
