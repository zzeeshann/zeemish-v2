<lesson-shell>

<lesson-beat name="hook">

You turn up the hot water tap. Thirty seconds later, you check the bathtub. The water level has dropped.

Your first thought: the drain must be open. You check. It's closed.

Here's what's actually happening. The cold tap is running. It's been running the whole time. And cold water is flowing out over the edge of the tub faster than hot water is flowing in from the tap you just opened.

You only saw one tap. You only thought about one flow. The system has two.

This is how most organizational problems work. You increase hiring and the backlog grows. You add budget and the project falls further behind. You work harder and feel more exhausted. The action is real. The result goes the wrong direction.

The bathtub doesn't care what you meant to do. It only responds to the difference between what's coming in and what's going out.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-1">

## Stocks and flows

A stock is an accumulation. The water in the bathtub. The amount that's sitting there at any moment.

A flow is a rate of change. The taps and the drain. The speed at which something moves in or out.

The stock only changes when inflows and outflows are unequal. If you're pouring in three liters per minute and draining two liters per minute, the stock grows by one liter per minute. If you're pouring in two and draining three, it shrinks by one. If both flows equal two, the level stays flat even though water is moving.

Three examples:

Your bank account is a stock. Your salary is an inflow. Your rent, groceries, subscriptions, coffee — those are outflows. The account balance changes based on the difference. If you get a raise but your expenses grow faster, the stock still shrinks. This is why people making six figures sometimes have less savings than people making half that.

Inventory is a stock. Production is an inflow. Sales are an outflow. A warehouse can be manufacturing more units than ever and still run out of product if customers are buying faster than the factory produces. The flow you're celebrating (production up 15%) can coincide with the stock you're panicking about (inventory at zero).

Your knowledge of Spanish is a stock. Learning new words is an inflow. Forgetting them is an outflow. If you studied 50 words last week but forgot 60, your vocabulary shrank. Time spent learning is not the same as knowledge accumulated. The stock is what remains after both flows have done their work.

Most people fixate on flows and ignore stocks. They talk about how much they're studying, how much they're hiring, how much they're investing — but they don't check whether the accumulation is actually changing. Or they fixate on one flow and pretend the other doesn't exist. They celebrate the new hires and never measure how many people are leaving. They track the water pouring in and never notice the open drain.

The bathtub makes this visible. In organizations, the stock is often invisible until it's a crisis.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-2">

## The delay problem

When you adjust a flow, the stock doesn't respond instantly. It accumulates or drains over time.

This breaks people's intuition. We expect cause and effect to be close together. Turn up the tap, water level rises. Hire more people, backlog shrinks. The gap between action and result is where most decisions fall apart.

Example: Your team has 40 people and a backlog of 200 tasks. You hire 20 more people in January. By March, you have 60 people and a backlog of 250 tasks. What happened?

Three possibilities, all invisible if you only watch the hiring flow:

First: people are leaving. You added 20 (inflow) but 15 people quit in the same period (outflow). Your net gain is 5, not 20. The backlog grows because the work inflow is still larger than your capacity.

Second: new hires aren't productive yet. They're onboarding. They're learning the system. For the first two months, they complete almost no tasks. Meanwhile, work keeps arriving at the old rate. The backlog grows even though headcount is rising. The stock (completed work capacity) lags behind the flow (new people) by months.

Third: the work inflow increased. Customers doubled their orders. Leadership committed to three new projects. Tasks are arriving faster than your expanded team can handle them. You're running faster and falling behind.

All three can be true at once. The hiring (inflow you control) feels like progress. The backlog (stock you're trying to change) tells a different story.

Another example: A reservoir sits at 40% capacity after two years of drought. Then it rains for three days straight. Inflow spikes. When the rain stops, the reservoir is at 38%.

Why? Two outflows grew faster than the rain could compensate.

Evaporation increased because the drought raised temperatures. More surface area, hotter air, faster evaporation. And downstream demand increased because farms and cities were desperately short on water. Authorities released more from the reservoir to keep taps running and crops alive.

The rain was real. Three days of storms added millions of liters. But the outflows were larger. The stock dropped.

People see the rain and expect the reservoir to fill. The gap between the action (more rain) and the result (lower water level) breaks their mental model. They assume the data is wrong, or someone made a mistake, or the system is rigged. The system is just doing math. Inflow minus outflow equals change in stock.

This delay — the time it takes for a change in flow to move the stock — is why interventions fail. You act on the flow you can see. You wait for the stock to respond. It doesn't, or it moves the wrong direction, and you assume your action didn't work. So you stop, or you double down on the wrong flow, or you give up entirely.

The stock is telling the truth. You're just reading the wrong part of the system.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="teaching-3">

## Why this breaks decisions

Most failed interventions come from adjusting one flow and ignoring the other, or expecting the stock to change instantly.

Climate example: Global CO2 emissions dropped 5.4% in 2020. Factories slowed, flights stopped, highways emptied. The inflow to atmospheric CO2 fell for the first time in decades.

Atmospheric CO2 concentration still rose. It went from 412 parts per million in 2019 to 414 in 2020.

Why? Because we're still adding CO2 faster than oceans and forests can remove it. The outflow (natural absorption) is maybe 50% of the inflow on a good year. Cutting emissions by 5% means we're pouring in 95% of the previous rate. The stock still grows, just slightly slower.

People saw emissions fall and expected CO2 to fall. The stock doesn't respond to flows in isolation. It responds to the difference. As long as inflow exceeds outflow, the bathtub fills. Slower filling is still filling.

Personal example: You start running three times a week. Inflow to cardiovascular fitness goes up. You expect to feel stronger, sleep better, handle stress more easily.

Two months later, you feel worse. More tired. Easier to irritate. What happened?

Stress at work doubled. You're sleeping five hours a night instead of seven. You're skipping meals because you're busy. These are outflows from fitness — things that drain your recovery capacity faster than exercise builds it.

The running is working. The inflow is real. But the outflows are larger, so the stock (your actual functional fitness) is dropping. You're pouring water into a tub with the drain open, and blaming the tap.

The fix isn't to run more. The fix is to map both flows before you act.

If you only measure what's coming in, you can't explain why the stock isn't changing. If you only adjust inflows, you waste effort fighting an invisible outflow. If you expect stocks to respond instantly to flow changes, you'll quit before the accumulation has time to show up.

Systems thinkers draw the bathtub first. They label the stock. They identify every inflow and every outflow. They measure the rates. Then they look for the highest-leverage change — the flow that's largest, or easiest to adjust, or most out of balance.

Most people skip straight to "turn up the tap" because it feels like action. The bathtub doesn't care about your feelings. It's still doing the same math it always does.

</lesson-beat>

<lesson-beat name="practice">

## Draw your bathtub

Pick one problem you're trying to solve right now. Something where your actions aren't producing the results you expect.

Draw a simple bathtub diagram. You don't need software. Paper works.

Label the stock. This is the thing you're trying to change. The accumulation. Examples: your savings, your team's morale, your company's reputation, your fitness, the number of bugs in your codebase, your fluency in a language.

Label the inflows. What increases the stock? List everything that adds to it. Be specific. "Income" is vague. "Salary, freelance projects, interest from savings account" is specific.

Label the outflows. What decreases the stock? Again, list everything. If the stock is team morale, outflows might include: boring work, unclear priorities, managers who don't listen, long commutes, pay below market rate.

Now write two things:

Which flow have you been focused on? The one you've been trying to adjust, the one you talk about in meetings, the one you're measuring.

Which flow have you been ignoring? The one you haven't measured, or haven't noticed, or assumed was too hard to change.

You don't need to solve the problem yet. Just see the whole system. See both taps, see the drain, see the stock.

Most of the time, this is enough to explain why your results don't match your effort.
